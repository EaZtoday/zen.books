# 1099 for Contractors: What Every Landlord Needs to Know

*Every January I get panicked calls from landlords who just realized they were supposed to issue 1099s to their contractors. Let's make sure that's not you this year.*

---

> **The short version:** If you paid any contractor $600 or more for work on your rental properties, you're required to issue a 1099-NEC by January 31. Collect W-9s before you pay anyone, track payments in QuickBooks by vendor, and [run a vendor report](/blog/fix-and-flip-accounting) in December so January isn't a scramble.

Here's the scene. It's January 20th. You're pulling together your tax documents. Your CPA asks: "Did you issue 1099s to your contractors?" And your stomach drops because you have no idea what they're talking about.

You paid a handyman $4,000 over the course of the year. A plumber got $1,200 for two jobs. Your property manager collected fees every month. And you didn't issue a single 1099 to any of them.

This happens constantly. Let's fix it.

---

## Do Landlords Need to Issue 1099s?

**Yes — if you paid someone $600 or more for services, you're required to issue a 1099-NEC.** If you're a landlord or real estate investor operating as a business — and if you own rental property, you are — this applies to you.

This applies whether you own one rental or fifty. It applies whether your properties are in LLCs or in your personal name.

If you paid someone to do work on your rentals, and that payment totals $600 or more for the year, you probably owe them a 1099.

---

## Who Gets a 1099-NEC?

**Here's the list of people and businesses that typically need to receive a 1099-NEC from a landlord:**

- **Handymen and general contractors** (sole proprietors, LLCs taxed as partnerships or sole props)
- **Plumbers, electricians, HVAC techs** (if not incorporated)
- **Property managers** — yes, your management company gets a 1099 for the fees you paid them
- **Landscapers and snow removal** crews
- **Cleaning crews** between tenants
- **Attorneys** — always get a 1099, even if they're incorporated (this is one of the exceptions)
- **Bookkeepers and accountants** (if paid $600+, not through payroll)

**Who does NOT get a 1099:**

- **Corporations** (S-corps and C-corps are generally exempt, except for attorneys)
- **Payments made via credit card or payment apps** (PayPal, Venmo for business, etc.) — the payment processor handles the 1099-K reporting
- **W-2 employees** — they get a W-2, not a 1099
- **Material suppliers** — you bought lumber from Home Depot, that's a product purchase, not a service

The distinction matters. You don't need to issue a 1099 to the hardware store. You do need to issue one to the guy you paid $800 cash to paint a unit.

## The W-9 Problem

**This is where most landlords mess up, and it's the easiest thing to prevent.**

A W-9 is the form you collect from a contractor that gives you their legal name, business name, address, and tax ID number (EIN or SSN). You need this information to file their 1099.

### Collect W-9s before you pay — not in January

**Collect the W-9 BEFORE you pay them.** Not in January when you're scrambling. Before the first check goes out.

Make it part of your onboarding process. New contractor? Great — here's a W-9, fill it out before we start. Keep them organized in a folder (digital or physical).

When January comes around, you already have everything you need.

> **Key takeaway:** Trying to track down a handyman's tax ID in January — six months after the job was done — is painful. Half the time the phone number doesn't work anymore. Collect the W-9 on day one and you'll never have this problem.

## The Deadline and Penalties

**The deadline is January 31.** That's the date by which 1099-NEC forms must be:
1. Sent to the recipient (the contractor)
2. Filed with the IRS

Miss it and you're looking at penalties:

- **$60 per form** if filed within 30 days of the deadline
- **$130 per form** if filed by August 1
- **$310 per form** if filed after August 1 or not at all

### Flippers: the penalties stack fast

If you're [flipping houses](/fix-and-flip), you might be issuing dozens of these — our [fix-and-flip accounting guide](/blog/fix-and-flip-accounting) covers how to stay organized across multiple deals with multiple contractors.

Those penalties add up fast. Five contractors, all late? That's $1,550 if you don't file at all. For something that takes maybe two hours to do properly if your books are organized.

## Common Mistakes

**These are the ones I see over and over — and every one of them is avoidable.**

### Issuing 1099s to corporations

If a contractor is an S-corp or C-corp, they're generally exempt. That's why the W-9 matters — it tells you their entity type. Don't waste time (and don't confuse your contractors) by issuing unnecessary forms.

### Forgetting property management fees

Your property manager collects rent, takes their 8-10% cut, and sends you the rest. Those management fees are payments for services. They get a 1099.

### Missing the $600 threshold across multiple payments

You paid your handyman $200 three times over the year. That's $600 total. It counts. Track cumulative payments per vendor, not just individual checks.

### Not issuing to attorneys

Attorneys always get a 1099, regardless of their business structure. This is a specific IRS exception. If you paid a real estate attorney $600+ for closings, lease reviews, or evictions — they get a form.

### Paying cash and not tracking it

If you paid someone cash for a job, that payment still counts toward the $600 threshold. And if you don't have a record of it, you can't issue the 1099 accurately. Always document cash payments.

> **Key takeaway:** Most 1099 mistakes come from not having a W-9 on file. If you know the contractor's entity type before you pay them, you already know whether a 1099 is required.

## How to Make This Easy

**The fix is simple — build the process into your bookkeeping, not your January panic.**

### Keep a vendor list in QuickBooks

Add every contractor's name, address, and tax ID (from their W-9) to your vendor list. When you record a payment to a contractor, it goes against their vendor profile.

### Run a vendor payment report in December

Sort by total payments. Anyone over $600 who isn't a corporation gets a 1099. The report takes five minutes to pull if your books are clean.

### Use QuickBooks' built-in 1099 tracking

QBO can flag vendors as 1099-eligible and generate the forms at year-end. You just need to set it up correctly from the start.

Whether you're a [buy-and-hold landlord](/buy-and-hold) or running flips, this is something your bookkeeper should be handling year-round — not scrambling in January.
