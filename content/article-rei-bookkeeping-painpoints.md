# 5 Bookkeeping Mistakes That Cost Real Estate Investors Thousands Every Year

*And how to fix them before your next tax season.*

---

You closed deals this year. You collected rent. You renovated properties. But when it comes to your books — you have no idea where you actually stand.

You're not alone. Most real estate investors we talk to are making the same costly mistakes with their bookkeeping. Here are the five we see over and over — and what to do about each one.

---

## 1. You Have No Idea How Much Each Property Is Actually Making You

You know your portfolio is "doing okay" — but can you pull up a profit-and-loss statement for 123 Elm Street right now? Most investors can't.

Without per-property tracking, you're flying blind. You don't know which rentals are cash-flowing and which ones are quietly bleeding you dry. That "good deal" you bought two years ago? It might be your worst performer — but you'd never know without the numbers broken out.

**The fix:** Every property needs its own P&L. Income, expenses, mortgage, maintenance — all tracked separately. That's the only way to make real decisions about what to hold, what to sell, and where to invest next. We wrote a full guide on [per-property P&L tracking](/blog/per-property-pl-tracking) — start there.

---

## 2. Your Bookkeeper Doesn't Understand That a New Roof Isn't a "Repair"

This one is expensive. A general bookkeeper sees a $15,000 roof replacement and categorizes it as "Repairs & Maintenance." But in real estate, that's a capital expenditure — it gets depreciated over time, not expensed in one year.

The difference? Thousands of dollars in tax liability. And it goes both ways — sometimes expenses that *should* be deducted immediately get capitalized instead.

**The fix:** Your bookkeeper needs to understand the difference between repairs (fixing what's broken) and improvements (adding value or extending useful life). If they don't speak real estate, they're costing you money every single quarter. We break down the exact rules in our guide to [CapEx vs. repairs classification](/blog/capex-vs-repairs).

---

## 3. You're Years Behind and the Thought of Tax Season Makes You Sick

We hear this one constantly. You meant to stay on top of it. Then one month slipped, then three, then a full year. Now you've got two or three years of bank statements, receipts in shoeboxes, and a growing sense of dread every time April approaches.

The longer you wait, the worse it gets — and the more it costs to clean up. But the real cost isn't the cleanup fee. It's the deductions you're missing, the penalties you're risking, and the stress that follows you every single day.

**The fix:** Rip off the band-aid. A proper catch-up engagement gets you current, reconciled, and ready for your CPA. It's never as bad as you think — but it's always worse the longer you wait.

---

## 4. You're Running Multiple Entities but Treating Them Like One

You've got an acquisition LLC, a holding company, maybe a property management entity. Smart structure — but if your books don't reflect it, you're creating a mess that could cost you your liability protection.

Commingling funds between entities, missing intercompany transfers, or just dumping everything into one QuickBooks file defeats the entire purpose of having separate LLCs. And if the IRS or a lawsuit ever comes knocking, sloppy books can pierce that corporate veil faster than anything.

**The fix:** Each entity needs its own clean set of books. Transfers between entities need to be recorded properly. It's not complicated — it just needs to be done right from the start. Our guide to [multi-entity LLC bookkeeping](/blog/multi-entity-llc-bookkeeping) walks through the right way to structure this.

---

## 5. You Don't Know Your True ROI on Marketing Spend

You're spending $3,000 a month on direct mail, $1,500 on PPC, and $800 on driving for dollars. But which channel is actually bringing you deals?

Most investors track their deal flow by gut feeling. "I think most of our deals come from direct mail." Thinking isn't knowing. Without tracking marketing spend against actual closed deals, you're burning cash on channels that might not be working — and underinvesting in the ones that are.

**The fix:** Track every marketing dollar by channel, tie it to lead source, and connect it all the way through to closed deals and actual profit. That's how you turn marketing from a cost center into a growth engine.
