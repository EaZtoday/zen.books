# Bookkeeping When You're Running 5 Flips at Once: How to Scale Without Losing Your Mind

*One deal is simple. Five is a different job entirely.*

---

> **The short version:** The bookkeeping system that works for your first two or three flips will collapse under the weight of four or five. Not because you're doing anything wrong — but because the system was never designed for that volume. Here's what the structure needs to look like when you're scaling, and why most investors wait too long to build it.

I hear a version of this story regularly.

A flipper starts out doing one or two deals a year. They manage the books themselves — a spreadsheet, maybe a basic QuickBooks setup. It's messy, but manageable. Their CPA sorts it out at year-end.

They scale. Three deals become five. Five becomes seven. And somewhere around deal four or five, the wheels come off. They can't tell you what each project has spent. They're not sure if the contractor invoice they paid last week went to the right job. The books from January through March are a blur. Tax season becomes a reckoning.

The problem isn't them. The problem is they never updated the system.

---

## Why Volume Changes Everything

When you're running one flip at a time, every transaction is obviously connected to one project. There's no ambiguity.

When you're running five simultaneously, every transaction needs to be:
- Correctly attributed to the right project
- Properly categorized within that project (acquisition cost? rehab expense? holding cost? financing cost?)
- Recorded in the right time period
- Reconciled against the right bank account or credit card

Multiply that by the transaction volume of five active rehabs — contractor draws, material receipts, insurance payments, utility bills, loan payments, permit fees — and you're looking at potentially 50-100 transactions per month, each one requiring a judgment call.

Without structure, things go wrong fast. Costs get attributed to the wrong project. The budget vs. actual on Project 3 is fiction. You can't tell if you're making money on the Scranton deal because the books don't separate it from the Dunmore deal.

## The System That Scales: What It Actually Looks Like

### Dedicated Project Tracking in QuickBooks

The core of a scalable flip bookkeeping system is **project-based accounting** — where every transaction is tagged to a specific deal from day one.

In QuickBooks Online Plus, this is the Projects feature. Every active flip gets its own Project. Every transaction — from the acquisition wire to the final commission check at closing — gets tagged to that Project.

What this gives you:
- A running cost total per deal, updated in real time
- Budget vs. actual visibility per project
- Projected margin before you close
- A clean, audit-ready record for each deal after it sells

If you're using classes alongside Projects, you can also track by entity (LLC) when you're operating multiple LLCs — [which is its own level of complexity](/blog/multi-entity-llc-bookkeeping).

### Separate Bank Accounts Per Deal (or Per Entity)

At scale, running all your deals through one business checking account is a nightmare. Transactions blur together. Reconciliation takes forever. You can't quickly pull a statement for just one deal.

The cleanest structure at 5+ deals:
- One operating account for overhead and business expenses
- Either a per-deal account, or a per-entity account if each deal is in its own LLC

Yes, this means more accounts to manage. But it means each deal's cash flow is clean and separable. When a hard money lender asks for a draw accounting or your CPA asks what came in and out of Deal #3, you can pull it in 60 seconds.

### A Transaction Processing Routine

At scale, "whenever I get around to it" becomes a liability. The system needs a routine:

**Weekly:**
- Import and categorize new transactions in all active project accounts
- Match any contractor invoices to transactions
- Flag anything uncategorized for review

**Monthly:**
- Reconcile all accounts
- Run a per-project cost summary
- Compare actuals to budget — flag any project that's running over by more than 10%
- Update the projected margin on each active deal

**At closing:**
- Enter the settlement statement line by line
- Close out the project account
- Reconcile the final proceeds against the books
- Generate the per-deal P&L for your CPA file

### A Standardized Chart of Accounts for Flips

One of the biggest scaling mistakes: letting the chart of accounts grow organically. By deal five, you have three different accounts for "contractor labor" because different things got entered different ways.

The chart of accounts needs to be locked in before you scale:
- Consistent account names and numbers across every project
- Clear distinction between acquisition costs, rehab/CapEx, holding costs, financing costs, and selling costs
- [CapEx vs. repair decisions documented per transaction](/blog/capex-vs-repairs) — not guessed at year-end

## The Staffing Question: When to Stop DIYing

There's a point where the math stops working on self-managed books.

If you're spending 10 hours per month on bookkeeping — and that's conservative at 5+ flips — and your time as a deal-finder is worth $150/hour, you're spending $1,500/month in opportunity cost on a task that a specialized bookkeeper handles for a fraction of that.

More importantly: a bookkeeper who does this daily will catch things you miss. The contractor invoice that doesn't match the draw schedule. The holding cost that got double-entered. The closing statement line item that should have gone to a different account.

At three or more simultaneous flips, the bookkeeping complexity has usually outgrown a self-managed system. That's not a judgment — it's a function of transaction volume and the level of detail that accurate flip accounting requires.

> **The inflection point I see most often:** Investors wait until they've had a bad tax year — one with a big unexpected bill, or one where the CPA had to reconstruct the books from scratch — before they build a real system. You can build the system proactively instead.

## What to Look for in a Bookkeeper at Scale

Not every bookkeeper understands fix-and-flip. Most don't. When you're interviewing someone to manage the books on five simultaneous deals, ask:

- **"How do you handle hard money draws in QuickBooks?"** — They should know to record the loan as a liability, not as income.
- **"How do you track cost basis vs. operating expenses for a flip?"** — They should understand the difference and why it matters.
- **"Do you use Projects in QuickBooks?"** — If they don't know what you're talking about, they haven't done project-based accounting.
- **"What does your month-end process look like for an active flip?"** — They should have a clear answer.
