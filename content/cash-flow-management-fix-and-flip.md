# Cash Flow Management for Fix-and-Flip Investors: Why Your Books Need to Predict, Not Just Record

*Profit and cash flow are not the same thing. Flippers who confuse them run out of money on profitable deals.*

---

> **The short version:** Fix-and-flip is a cash-intensive business with uneven, unpredictable cash flows. Hard money draws arrive in chunks. Contractor payments go out in lumps. You might be sitting on a paper profit while your checking account is nearly empty. Standard bookkeeping records what happened. Cash flow management shows you what's about to happen — so you're not surprised. Here's how to build it.

I've talked to flippers who are profitable on paper and cash-strapped in reality.

The deal math works: they bought right, the rehab stayed on budget, the ARV is solid. On paper, there's $40,000 in projected profit. But the contractor needs $18,000 for phase two, the hard money interest payment is due next week, the second property they just closed needs materials, and the first property hasn't sold yet.

The money exists — it's just tied up in assets. And right now, they need cash.

This is a cash flow problem, not a profitability problem. And the difference matters a lot.

---

## Why Fix-and-Flip Cash Flow Is Uniquely Difficult

Most business cash flow is relatively predictable. You have recurring revenue, recurring expenses, and the timing is reasonably consistent.

Flipping doesn't work that way:

**Money goes out in large, irregular chunks.** The earnest money deposit. The down payment at closing. Phase draws as the contractor hits milestones. The holding costs that accumulate silently in the background. And then more cash out at closing on the sale side — prorations, commissions, payoffs.

**Money comes back all at once.** Your revenue event is a single wire transfer when the deal closes. Until then, you're entirely cash-out.

**Multiple deals create overlapping cash events.** If you're running three simultaneous flips, you might have two contractor draws, a hard money payment, a property tax bill, and an insurance renewal all hitting in the same week — with no closing proceeds expected for another 30 days.

**Timelines slip.** The rehab that was supposed to take 8 weeks takes 14. The property that was supposed to list in March lists in May. Every week of slippage adds holding costs and pushes the cash recovery event further out.

## The Two Levels of Cash Flow Management

### Level 1: Cash Position Visibility (Bookkeeping)

This is the baseline: knowing exactly where you are right now.

Your books should give you, in real time:
- Total cash on hand across all accounts
- Outstanding contractor invoices not yet paid
- Upcoming loan payments (hard money monthly interest, balloon payments)
- Insurance and tax bills due in the next 30 days
- Committed project expenses not yet drawn

If your books are running two weeks behind because you're entering transactions manually once a month, you don't have cash position visibility — you have a historical record that's already obsolete.

> **The fix:** Sync all accounts (business checking, business credit cards, loan accounts) to QuickBooks. Reconcile weekly, not monthly. With real-time sync, your cash position is accurate within 24-48 hours of any transaction.

### Level 2: Cash Flow Forecasting (Planning)

This is where it gets more useful: projecting forward 30, 60, and 90 days.

For each active project, this means modeling out:
- **Remaining draws:** What phases are left? What will each cost? When will they hit?
- **Remaining holding costs:** How many more weeks/months until close? What's the burn rate?
- **Remaining financing costs:** Hard money payments remaining until the balloon or payoff
- **Expected proceeds:** ARV minus selling costs minus loan payoff = net to you, on what timeline?

Stack this across all active projects, and you get a consolidated 90-day cash flow picture. You can see the weeks where cash will be tight before they happen — and plan accordingly.

## The Hard Money Tracking Problem

Hard money loans are the engine of fix-and-flip cash flow — and they're the most commonly misrecorded item I see in flipper books.

The typical flow:
1. Hard money lender funds the acquisition and usually a portion of the rehab
2. Rehab draws come from a draw schedule — you request funds, they inspect, they release
3. Interest accrues monthly (or is deducted from draws, depending on the lender)
4. At closing, the full loan balance (principal + any accrued interest) gets paid off from proceeds

**How this should be recorded:**
- The initial loan is a liability on your balance sheet — not income, not equity
- Each draw is a liability increase — money comes in, loan balance goes up
- Monthly interest is an expense (or a holding cost added to basis — ask your CPA)
- The payoff at closing reduces the liability to zero

**How it often gets recorded:**
- The draws are entered as income or as a deposit
- Interest payments are buried in "miscellaneous expenses"
- The payoff is entered as a single large expense at closing

When draws are recorded as income, your P&L looks wildly off. When interest is misclassified, you lose the deduction or miscalculate your basis. When the payoff isn't properly reconciled, your balance sheet has a phantom liability sitting on it.

## Building a Simple Cash Flow Projection

You don't need a sophisticated model to get visibility. Here's the working version:

**For each active deal, build a table:**

| Week | Cash Out | Cash In | Net | Running Balance |
|------|----------|---------|-----|-----------------|
| Week 1 | Contractor draw $12,000 | — | -$12,000 | $28,000 |
| Week 2 | Hard money interest $1,800 | — | -$1,800 | $26,200 |
| Week 3 | Materials $3,400 | — | -$3,400 | $22,800 |
| Week 4 | — | — | — | $22,800 |
| Week 6 | Final contractor $8,000 | — | -$8,000 | $14,800 |
| Week 8 | — | Close proceeds $47,000 | +$47,000 | $61,800 |

Consolidate this across all active projects and you see your cash position by week. You can spot the tight weeks before they arrive.

## Knowing When You Can Take the Next Deal

Cash flow management isn't just about surviving the current projects — it's about making intelligent decisions on the next ones.

Before you commit to a new deal, you should know:
- What your current cash position is
- What your projected cash position will be at the point you need to put earnest money down and close
- Whether you have the reserves to cover the down payment, initial costs, and first phase draws without creating a crisis in your existing projects

A lot of investors make this decision based on vibes and hope. The ones who scale reliably make it based on numbers.

> **The real estate professional test:** Can you answer "what will my checking account look like in 60 days?" If not, you're flying blind. The system to answer that question takes a few hours to build correctly. Once it exists, it becomes part of your normal monthly review.

## What Your Books Need to Enable This

None of this works without clean, current books as the foundation:
- Every account synced and reconciled weekly
- Every active deal tracked as its own project with running costs
- [Holding costs tracked at the deal level](/blog/holding-costs-house-flip), not lumped into general overhead
- Hard money loans properly structured as liabilities, with draw history
- A monthly review cadence that produces a consolidated view across all active deals
