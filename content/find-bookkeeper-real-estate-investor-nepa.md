# How to Find a Bookkeeper Who Actually Understands Real Estate Investing (NEPA Edition)

*Most bookkeepers are competent at small business accounting. Real estate investing is a different structure — and the investors who get the most out of their books find someone who knows that difference.*

---

> **The short version:** Five questions will tell you whether a bookkeeper actually understands real estate investing or is learning on your dime. Ask them before you sign anything. The right bookkeeper sets up your QuickBooks correctly from day one — the wrong one creates cleanup work that costs more than the bookkeeping itself.

---

## Why Real Estate Bookkeeping Is Different

Most bookkeepers are trained to handle small businesses: service companies, retail, restaurants. Real estate investing has a different structure — and a bookkeeper who hasn't worked extensively with investors will set things up in ways that make sense for retail, not for a portfolio with multiple properties and entities.

Common setups we've cleaned up when investors come to us:

- **Rehab costs expensed as repairs** instead of capitalized as improvements (or vice versa)
- **Hard money loan draws recorded as income** instead of liability
- **Security deposits recorded as rental income** instead of held in a liability account
- **Flip sale proceeds recorded as revenue** without netting acquisition and improvement costs
- **All properties combined in one P&L** instead of tracked individually

These aren't unusual — they're exactly what you'd expect from a bookkeeper who was trying their best without real estate-specific training. The good news is they're fixable.

## What to Look For: 5 Non-Negotiables

> **Key takeaway:** The five questions below aren't trick questions — a bookkeeper with genuine REI experience will answer them quickly and specifically. Vague answers ("I've worked with a few landlords") are a signal to keep looking.

### 1. QuickBooks Online Proficiency — Not Just "QuickBooks"

QuickBooks Desktop and QuickBooks Online are different products. For real estate investors in 2025, QuickBooks Online is the standard: cloud access, bank feeds, accountant access, and mobile receipt capture. Make sure any bookkeeper you hire works primarily in QBO and can set up your chart of accounts correctly from day one.

Ask: *"Do you work in QuickBooks Online? Can you show me a sample chart of accounts you've set up for a rental property portfolio?"*

### 2. Experience with Real Estate Specifically

Not "business bookkeeping." Not "I've helped a few landlords." Specifically: rental property bookkeeping, fix-and-flip project tracking, multi-entity LLC structures, and depreciation schedules.

Ask: *"What percentage of your clients are real estate investors? Can you walk me through how you'd track a fix-and-flip project from acquisition to sale?"*

### 3. Understanding of CapEx vs. Repair Rules

This is the single most consequential bookkeeping issue for real estate investors, and the one most often botched. A competent bookkeeper should be able to explain the IRS's Tangible Property Regulations — or at least know what they are — and make defensible, consistent decisions about how to classify your expenses.

Ask: *"If I replace the HVAC system in a rental property I've owned for five years, how do you classify that? What if I replace a broken window?"*

### 4. Per-Property P&L Tracking

You should be able to see profit and loss by individual property — not just a total for your whole portfolio. This is table stakes for real estate bookkeeping, but many generic bookkeepers don't set this up correctly in QuickBooks.

Ask: *"How do you set up QuickBooks so I can see income and expenses by property? Can you show me a sample report?"*

### 5. 1099 Preparation and Contractor Tracking

Real estate investors pay contractors constantly. A good bookkeeper tracks every vendor payment through QuickBooks, collects W-9s, and prepares your 1099-NECs in January without you having to do anything. Many generic bookkeepers skip this or don't know how.

Ask: *"How do you handle 1099 preparation for my contractors? What's your process for tracking vendor payments and collecting W-9s?"*

## Red Flags to Walk Away From

- **"I've worked with a few landlords before"** — landlord bookkeeping ≠ real estate investor bookkeeping. Ask specifically about flippers, multi-entity structures, and project tracking.
- **Hourly billing without a clear scope** — a competent real estate bookkeeper should be able to give you a flat monthly rate based on your transaction volume. Hourly billing incentivizes inefficiency.
- **No access to your QBO file** — you should always have owner access to your own QuickBooks file. A bookkeeper who won't give you that is a red flag.
- **"I'll have it ready for tax season"** — monthly bookkeeping is the standard. Annual or quarterly catch-up doesn't give you the real-time visibility you need to manage a real estate portfolio.

- **$200–$400/month** for a small portfolio (1–5 properties, low flip volume)

Investors who try to go below these rates typically end up with books that require a full cleanup (at $1,500–$3,000+ for catch-up work) within 12–18 months. The math usually favors doing it right the first time.

A bookkeeper who understands these specifics will set up your chart of accounts correctly from day one. One who doesn't will ask you to explain it — and charge you for the education.
