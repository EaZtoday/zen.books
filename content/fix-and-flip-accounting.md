# Fix-and-Flip Accounting: What Most Bookkeepers Get Wrong From Day One

*Flips are not rental properties. The bookkeeping is completely different. If your books don't reflect that, you're guessing at your profit on every deal.*

---

I get calls from house flippers all the time who've been working with a general bookkeeper — someone who handles their taxes, maybe does their payroll — and they have no idea what their actual profit was on their last three flips.

They know what they bought the house for. They know roughly what they spent on rehab. They know what they sold it for. But the *actual* number — after holding costs, financing, marketing, commissions, closing costs, contractor overruns — is a mystery.

That's not a business. That's a series of gut-feeling bets.

Here's what real fix-and-flip bookkeeping looks like.

---

## Why Flips Are Different from Rentals

With rental property bookkeeping, you're tracking ongoing income and expenses across time. The property exists indefinitely. The books are organized around properties and months.

With fix-and-flip bookkeeping, you're tracking a *project*. It has a beginning (acquisition), a middle (rehab), and an end (sale). Everything is organized around the deal — not the property address, not the month.

This is the core mistake general bookkeepers make. They treat flip properties like rental properties. They categorize expenses as they come in and think that's sufficient. It's not. You need project-level tracking that lets you see the full P&L on each deal from start to finish.

---

## The Deal-Level P&L

Every flip should have its own profit and loss statement that looks roughly like this:

**Revenues:**
- Sale price

**Cost of Goods Sold:**
- Acquisition price
- Purchase closing costs (title, transfer taxes, recording fees)
- Rehab costs (broken down by category — see below)
- Holding costs (mortgage/hard money interest, property taxes, insurance, utilities)
- Selling costs (agent commissions, seller-paid closing costs, staging)
- Carrying costs (HOA fees if applicable)

**Gross Profit on Deal**

**Operating Expenses (allocated to deal):**
- Marketing (if you found this deal through direct mail or PPC, that cost belongs here)
- Administrative and professional fees (attorney, bookkeeper, estimated portion)

**Net Profit on Deal**

When you have this for every flip, you know your actual ROI — not your estimated one. And you can compare deals to understand which types of projects are your most profitable.

## Rehab Cost Tracking Done Right

The rehab budget is where most flip books fall apart.

You're writing checks and swiping cards constantly during a rehab. Contractor draws, materials runs at the hardware store, permits, dumpster rentals, inspections. If these all just go into one bucket called "Rehab Costs," you lose the ability to:

- Compare actual vs. budgeted costs by category
- Understand where your overruns happened
- Build better estimates for future projects
- Identify which contractors are reliable on budget vs. which ones aren't

Here's a better rehab breakdown:

**By trade/category:**
- Demo and hauling
- Framing and carpentry
- Plumbing
- Electrical
- HVAC
- Roofing
- Siding and exterior
- Flooring
- Drywall and paint
- Kitchen (cabinets, counters, appliances)
- Bathrooms
- Landscaping and curb appeal
- Permits and inspections

When you track at this level, your next estimate isn't a guess — it's based on actual data from your previous projects.

## Hard Money and Private Lending

Most flippers use financing. And the accounting around that financing is one of the most commonly mishandled areas.

**Interest expense.** Hard money interest is a holding cost that belongs in your deal P&L, not in a general "interest expense" bucket. Every month the deal is open, you're accruing that cost against the project.

**Points paid at close.** Those origination points are part of your acquisition cost, not a separate expense. They affect your all-in cost basis.

**Draw requests and reconciliation.** If your hard money lender holds back rehab funds in escrow and releases them in draws, those draws need to be tracked carefully. The money isn't yours until it's drawn, and the budget-to-actual needs to reconcile with what the lender approved.

## The Contractor 1099 Problem

At year-end, you need to issue 1099-NEC forms to any contractor or vendor you paid $600 or more during the year who isn't a corporation.

If your books are organized by project rather than by vendor, pulling this information at year-end is a disaster. Your bookkeeper has to go back through every deal, find every contractor payment, and hope the vendor names and addresses are recorded correctly.

The fix is simple: maintain a contractor list with W-9 information for every person or entity you hire. Log their payments as you go. Don't wait until January to figure out who you owe a 1099.

---

## The Inventory Accounting Question

Here's something most flippers don't think about: the IRS may classify you as a *dealer* rather than an *investor* depending on how frequently you flip and whether it's your primary business activity.

Dealers treat flip properties as inventory — like a car dealer treats cars. They don't get the favorable long-term capital gains rates that investors get. Their properties are subject to self-employment tax. And the accounting is fundamentally different.

This isn't something to figure out at tax time. It's something your bookkeeper and CPA should be thinking about as you scale your flipping business.

I'm not a CPA, so I'm not giving tax advice here. But I am saying: make sure your books are organized in a way that gives your CPA what they need to make this determination clearly.

---

## What the Numbers Should Tell You

When your fix-and-flip accounting is set up correctly, here's what you should be able to see at any point:

**Per deal:** Total acquisition cost, total rehab spend (vs. budget), current holding costs, projected total cost, projected gross profit, projected ROI.

**Portfolio-wide (active deals):** How much capital is deployed across all active flips, which deals are behind schedule (and therefore running up holding costs), which deals are over budget.

**Historical:** Average acquisition cost, average rehab cost, average hold time, average gross margin, average net profit per deal — broken down by market area, deal source, or property type.

That's a real business with real data. That's how you scale intelligently instead of just doing more deals and hoping more volume fixes the math.
