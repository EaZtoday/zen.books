# Fix-and-Flip Tax Deductions Most Investors Miss (And Why Clean Books Are the Difference)

*The deductions are there. The question is whether your books are built to capture them.*

---

> **The short version:** Most flippers know about rehab costs. Far fewer properly capture financing costs, soft costs, holding expenses, and business overhead in a way that's both accurate and defensible to the IRS. Here's the complete checklist — and the books structure that makes sure none of it slips through.

Here's a conversation I have at least twice a month.

A flipper brings me their books from last year — sometimes a spreadsheet, sometimes a QuickBooks file that hasn't been touched since their general bookkeeper set it up. They want to know if everything looks right before it goes to their CPA.

Every single time, I find the same gaps. Not fraud. Not sloppiness. Just deductions that are legitimately theirs that never got captured because the books weren't built to capture them.

Let me show you what I find.

## The Full Deduction Map for a Fix-and-Flip

Most flippers think their deductions are: purchase price + rehab costs = basis. Gross proceeds minus basis = taxable profit. Done.

It's more complicated — and more favorable to you — than that.

---

### Category 1: Acquisition Costs

Beyond the purchase price, several costs at closing become part of your cost basis and reduce your taxable gain:

- **Title search and title insurance** — both owner's and lender's policy premiums
- **Attorney fees** at closing
- **Recording fees** and transfer taxes
- **Survey costs**
- **Real estate agent commissions** paid on the acquisition side
- **Buyer's due diligence costs** — inspections, environmental assessments

> **Where people miss this:** These show up on your HUD-1/closing disclosure and then get ignored. If your bookkeeper isn't pulling each line from the settlement statement into your books as a basis addition, you're starting with an understated cost basis on day one.

---

### Category 2: Financing Costs

Hard money and private money loans come with significant costs that are deductible — but only if recorded correctly:

- **Origination fees and points** — these are financing costs, not closing costs, and the IRS treatment depends on your dealer vs. investor classification. [Understanding that classification matters here](/blog/real-estate-dealer-vs-investor-irs).
- **Loan origination fees** charged as a percentage of the loan amount
- **Broker fees** if you used a mortgage broker to place the loan
- **Prepayment penalties** if you paid off a hard money loan early
- **Monthly interest** on the hard money loan during the hold period

> **Where people miss this:** Hard money interest is often recorded as a lump sum when it's paid at closing, instead of being tracked month-by-month as a [holding cost](/blog/holding-costs-house-flip). This makes your monthly cost tracking inaccurate and can misstate your basis on deals that cross a tax year.

---

### Category 3: Holding Costs

Everything it costs to own the property between purchase and sale:

- **Property taxes** (prorated to your ownership period)
- **Property insurance** and builder's risk insurance premiums
- **Utilities** — electric, gas, water/sewer during the rehab
- **Trash removal and dumpster rentals**
- **HOA fees** if applicable
- **Security and alarm monitoring**
- **Lawn maintenance and snow removal** during the hold period
- **Storage unit costs** for materials on the property

> **Where people miss this:** Holding costs are often expensed in lump sums when paid, without being attributed to the specific project. For a flipper with two or three simultaneous deals, this makes it impossible to know what each flip actually cost to hold. [Proper per-deal tracking](/blog/holding-costs-house-flip) captures this correctly.

---

### Category 4: Rehab and Construction Costs

Most flippers track these — but not always completely:

- **Subcontractor labor and materials** — every invoice
- **General contractor overhead and profit**
- **Materials purchased directly** (not through the GC)
- **Equipment rentals** — lifts, compressors, specialty tools
- **Permits and inspection fees**
- **Architectural or engineering fees**
- **Structural drawings and blueprints**
- **Staging costs** if you stage before listing

> **Where people miss this:** Permits and professional fees (architect, engineer) often end up in a miscellaneous account instead of being tracked as capital expenditures that increase your cost basis. [Tracking rehab costs correctly in QuickBooks](/blog/track-rehab-costs-quickbooks) prevents this.

---

### Category 5: Selling Costs

These reduce your gross proceeds and directly reduce your taxable gain:

- **Real estate agent commissions** (typically 5-6% of sale price)
- **Seller closing costs** — transfer taxes, attorney fees, recording fees
- **Concessions to the buyer** — repairs negotiated after inspection, credits at closing
- **Staging and photography** if not already captured
- **Marketing costs** — MLS fees, professional photography, drone footage

> **Where people miss this:** Concessions and credits negotiated after an accepted offer often never make it into the books at all. If the buyer got a $4,000 credit for a new roof at closing, that needs to show up as a cost somewhere — or you're overstating your gain by $4,000.

---

### Category 6: Business Overhead

If you're doing this as a business (and if you're doing 3+ flips/year, you should be), you have deductible overhead:

- **Home office deduction** — the portion of your home used exclusively for the business, calculated correctly
- **Vehicle mileage** — every trip to the property, to meet with contractors, to the hardware store. At the IRS standard mileage rate, this adds up fast.
- **Cell phone** — the business-use percentage of your monthly plan
- **Professional services** — your bookkeeper (yes, us), your CPA, your real estate attorney
- **Education and training** — courses, books, subscriptions related to real estate investing
- **Software subscriptions** — your bookkeeping software, your property management tools
- **Business meals** with contractors, partners, potential joint venture partners (50% deductible)

> **Where people miss this:** Most flippers don't track mileage at all. A flipper doing 5 deals a year who visits each property 20 times has easily logged 1,000+ miles — at $0.67/mile in 2024, that's $670 in deductions from mileage alone. Keep a mileage log. Use MileIQ or just a notes app. It takes 30 seconds per trip.

## The Real Problem: Deductions That Can't Be Reconstructed

Every item on this list is legitimately yours — if the books capture it. If they don't, you have a problem.

Your CPA can only work with what you give them. If your closing statement from January never got entered into the books, those acquisition costs are gone at tax time. If your hard money interest got lumped into a "miscellaneous expenses" bucket with 30 other transactions, separating it accurately during tax prep is expensive and error-prone.

The deductions aren't the hard part. **The hard part is having books that capture every single one, correctly categorized, from the day you close on the property.**
