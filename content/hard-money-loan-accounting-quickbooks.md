# Hard Money Loan Tracking for Flips: How to Record Draws, Interest, and Points in QuickBooks

You close on a flip with hard money. The lender funds the purchase, holds back the rehab money in escrow, and charges you 2 points up front plus 12% annual interest. Three months later you're juggling draw requests, monthly interest payments, and a payoff balance that keeps shifting as more rehab funds get released.

Your bookkeeper records the monthly interest payment and calls it a day. The points? Buried in closing costs. The draws? Sitting in some catch-all account. And when the flip sells, nobody can tell you what this loan actually cost you.

That's how flippers end up thinking they made $35K on a deal when the real number was $22K. This guide shows you how to track every piece of a hard money loan in QuickBooks so your [deal-level P&L](/blog/fix-and-flip-accounting) tells the truth.

---

## The Moving Parts of a Hard Money Loan

Hard money loans aren't like a conventional mortgage. They have several cost components that all need to land in the right place on your books:

**Origination points.** Paid at closing, usually 1-3% of the loan amount. On a $200K loan, that's $2,000 to $6,000 right out the gate.

**Interest payments.** Monthly, interest-only during the hold period. At 12% on $200K, that's $2,000/month.

**Rehab draws.** The lender holds back the rehab budget and releases it in draws as work gets done. Each draw changes your loan balance.

**Extension fees.** If your flip takes longer than the original term, the lender charges an extension fee — often another point.

**Payoff at sale.** The loan gets paid off from closing proceeds. The payoff amount includes unpaid interest, the remaining principal, and any fees.

Every one of these hits your deal's bottom line. Miss any of them and your profit number is fiction.

## How to Set It Up in QuickBooks

**Step 1: Create a liability account for the loan.**

In your Chart of Accounts:
- Type: Other Current Liabilities (or Long Term Liabilities if the term is over 12 months)
- Name: "Hard Money Loan - 123 Main St" (name it by property)

Create a new liability account for each flip's financing. Don't lump multiple loans into one account.

**Step 2: Record the initial funding.**

When the lender funds the purchase:
- Debit: Bank Account (the purchase funds you received)
- Credit: Hard Money Loan liability (the amount funded)

If the lender also funded closing costs, those get recorded separately — title fees, transfer taxes, and recording fees each go to their appropriate expense accounts within the project.

**Step 3: Record origination points.**

Points are a financing cost. Record them as an expense within the project:
- Debit: "Loan Points / Origination Fees" expense (assigned to the flip project)
- Credit: Bank Account (if paid from your funds) or Hard Money Loan liability (if deducted from loan proceeds)

The points are part of your all-in cost on this deal. They belong in the deal P&L, not in a generic "interest expense" bucket. We see Scranton and Lehigh Valley flippers making this mistake constantly — burying points in general overhead where they disappear from the deal analysis.

---

## Tracking Monthly Interest

Each month the loan is open, you're paying interest. Record it as:
- Debit: "Hard Money Interest" expense (assigned to the flip project)
- Credit: Bank Account

Tag it to the project. This is a [holding cost](/blog/holding-costs-house-flip) — every month the deal sits open, this number grows. When you pull the project P&L, you should see exactly how much the money cost you on this specific deal.

If you're running multiple flips simultaneously, each flip's interest goes to its own project. That's how you figure out which deals are actually profitable after financing costs — and which ones only looked good before you accounted for the money.

---

## Recording Rehab Draws

This is where it gets tricky. The lender releases rehab funds in draws, usually after inspecting completed work.

When a draw is released:
- Debit: Bank Account (the draw amount deposited)
- Credit: Hard Money Loan liability (your loan balance just increased)

The draw itself isn't an expense — it's borrowed money hitting your account. The expense happens when you pay the contractor with those funds. That contractor payment gets coded to the appropriate [rehab cost category](/blog/track-rehab-costs-quickbooks) within the project.

Keep your draws reconciled against the lender's draw schedule. At any point, you should be able to say: "The lender has released 3 of 5 draws totaling $28,000 against a $45,000 rehab holdback."

---

## Extension Fees

If the flip takes longer than the original loan term and you need to extend, the extension fee is another financing cost:
- Debit: "Loan Extension Fee" expense (assigned to the project)
- Credit: Bank Account or Hard Money Loan liability

This is a signal, by the way. If you're paying extension fees regularly, your rehab timelines are off and it's eating your margins. Track these separately so you can see the pattern.

---

## Payoff at Closing

When the flip sells, the hard money loan gets paid off from closing proceeds. The settlement statement will show:

- Loan payoff amount (principal balance)
- Any accrued unpaid interest
- Any outstanding fees

Record the payoff:
- Debit: Hard Money Loan liability (zeroing out the balance)
- Debit: Hard Money Interest expense (for any accrued interest included in payoff)
- Credit: Bank Account (or the closing settlement)

After this entry, the loan liability should be zero. If it's not, you have a reconciliation issue — probably a draw that wasn't recorded or an interest payment that got miscoded. Fix it before you finalize the deal P&L.

## What Your Deal P&L Should Show

When everything is recorded correctly, your project P&L has a clear financing section:

| Financing Cost | Amount |
|---------------|--------|
| Origination Points (2 pts on $200K) | $4,000 |
| Monthly Interest (4 months × $2,000) | $8,000 |
| Extension Fee | $0 |
| **Total Financing Cost** | **$12,000** |

Now add that to your rehab costs, acquisition costs, and selling costs. That's your real all-in number. Not the one where financing "doesn't count."

On a deal with a $40K gross spread, $12K in financing costs is 30% of your profit gone. You need to see that number — clearly, per deal — to make smart decisions about how long to hold, which lenders to use, and whether the deal was actually worth doing.
