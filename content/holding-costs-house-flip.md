# Holding Costs That Kill Flip Margins: Track Them or Lose Money on Every Deal

You're four months into a flip. The rehab is almost done — just waiting on the electrician to come back and the county to schedule the final inspection. Meanwhile, the hard money lender is collecting $1,800 this month, the insurance is $200, the property taxes keep accruing, and the water bill just hit $85.

None of that feels like a big deal in isolation. But it's month four, and those "small" costs have been running since the day you closed on the purchase. Add it all up and you've spent $9,000 just to own this property while it sits empty during rehab.

That $9,000 comes directly off your profit. If you're not tracking holding costs per deal, you don't know what your flips actually make — and you definitely don't know which deals are bleeding you dry. This article breaks down every holding cost, shows you what they really add up to, and tells you how to track them in your books.

---

## What Are Holding Costs?

Holding costs are the expenses you pay simply for owning a property over time. They're not rehab costs (you'd pay those regardless of how long the project takes). They're time-based costs — the longer you hold, the more you pay.

For flippers, holding costs start the day you close on the purchase and stop the day you close on the sale. Every day in between is costing you money.

---

## The Full List

Here's every holding cost you need to track on a flip:

**Hard money or private lender interest.**
This is almost always the biggest holding cost. Hard money rates typically run 10-14% annual, interest-only. On a $180,000 loan at 12%, you're paying $1,800 per month. On a 5-month hold, that's $9,000 in interest alone.

If you're tracking your [hard money loan correctly](/blog/hard-money-loan-accounting-quickbooks), this number is easy to pull from QuickBooks. If you're not, it's buried somewhere in your bank statements.

**Property taxes.**
You owe property taxes from the day you take ownership. These accrue daily even if they're paid quarterly or annually. On a property with a $3,600 annual tax bill, that's $300/month or roughly $10/day.

Taxes get prorated at both purchase and sale closing. Your closing statement shows what you owe. Make sure these prorations get recorded in your [deal P&L](/blog/fix-and-flip-accounting).

**Insurance.**
You need a builder's risk or vacant property policy during rehab. Standard homeowner's insurance doesn't cover a property under active renovation. Expect $150-300/month depending on the property value and scope of work.

**Utilities.**
Your contractors need lights, running water, and sometimes heat or AC depending on the season. You're paying:
- Electric: $75-200/month
- Water/sewer: $50-100/month
- Gas (if applicable): $50-150/month in winter

In NEPA, a winter rehab means the gas bill can spike if you're running heat to keep pipes from freezing or to let drywall mud and paint dry properly. Scranton and Wilkes-Barre flippers know this one well.

**HOA fees.**
If the property is in an HOA (less common for flips, but it happens), those monthly dues are a holding cost.

**Lawn care and property maintenance.**
The grass still grows while you're rehabbing. Code enforcement doesn't care that you're mid-renovation — if the lawn is overgrown, you're getting a notice. Budget $50-100/month during growing season.

**Loan extension fees.**
If your hard money loan has a 6-month term and the flip takes 8 months, the extension fee (often another point on the loan) is a holding cost you directly caused by going over timeline.

## What Holding Costs Really Look Like

Here's a realistic example for a $180K flip in the Lehigh Valley:

| Holding Cost | Monthly | 4 Months | 6 Months |
|-------------|---------|----------|----------|
| Hard money interest (12% on $180K) | $1,800 | $7,200 | $10,800 |
| Property taxes ($3,600/yr) | $300 | $1,200 | $1,800 |
| Insurance | $200 | $800 | $1,200 |
| Electric | $125 | $500 | $750 |
| Water/sewer | $75 | $300 | $450 |
| Gas (winter rehab) | $100 | $400 | $600 |
| Lawn care (summer) | $75 | $300 | $450 |
| **Total** | **$2,675** | **$10,700** | **$16,050** |

The difference between a 4-month flip and a 6-month flip is $5,350 in holding costs. That's money that comes straight off your profit for no additional value — the same rehab, the same sale price, just two more months of ownership.

Now multiply that across three or four active flips. Every week of delay across your portfolio is costing you thousands.

## Why Flippers Underestimate Holding Costs

Three reasons:

**They don't track them per deal.** The hard money interest hits the bank account every month. The insurance gets paid. The utilities run. But if these expenses aren't assigned to the specific flip project in QuickBooks, they end up in general overhead. When you pull the deal P&L, the holding costs aren't there — so the deal looks more profitable than it is.

**They only think about rehab costs.** When evaluating a potential flip, most investors focus on the purchase price and the rehab budget. They mentally subtract those from the ARV and call the difference "profit." Holding costs get a vague "yeah, plus whatever the interest is" treatment. That vagueness costs real money.

**They underestimate timelines.** Your contractor says 8 weeks. It takes 14. That's six extra weeks of holding costs you didn't budget for. On the example above, six weeks of overrun costs $4,013.

## How to Track Holding Costs in QuickBooks

The setup is straightforward if you're already [tracking rehab costs by project](/blog/track-rehab-costs-quickbooks):

1. **Create holding cost sub-accounts** under your project cost accounts:
   - Hard Money Interest
   - Property Taxes (Holding Period)
   - Insurance (Holding Period)
   - Utilities - Electric
   - Utilities - Water
   - Utilities - Gas
   - Lawn/Property Maintenance

2. **Tag every holding cost to the project.** Each monthly payment gets assigned to the specific flip it belongs to.

3. **Review monthly.** Pull the project P&L once a month and look at the holding cost section. Is it where you expected? Is the deal still worth doing at the current pace?

---

## How to Reduce Holding Costs

You can't eliminate holding costs on a flip. But you can minimize them:

**Tighten your timelines.** The single biggest lever. A 3-month rehab vs. a 5-month rehab saves you $5,350 on the example above. Get permits pulled before closing. Have your contractor lined up and ready to start on day one.

**Negotiate better loan terms.** Shop hard money lenders. The difference between 10% and 14% on $180K is $600/month. Over a 5-month hold, that's $3,000.

**List fast.** Have your agent and photographer ready before the final punch list is done. Lost days between "rehab complete" and "listed on MLS" are pure holding cost with zero value.

**Know your break-even date.** Calculate the date at which holding costs eat your remaining profit margin. If your break-even is month 7 and you're in month 5 with no end in sight — it's time to reassess.
