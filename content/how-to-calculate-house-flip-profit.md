# How to Calculate True House Flip Profit (Not the Number You Tell Your Friends)

*You did the math on a napkin. Sale price minus purchase minus rehab equals profit. Except it doesn't — not even close.*

---

> **The short version:** True flip profit = Sale Price minus ALL costs: acquisition (purchase + closing + points), rehab (contractor + materials + permits), holding (interest + taxes + insurance + utilities), and selling (commissions + closing + concessions). Most flippers leave out 8-15% of actual costs. On a $200K sale, that's $16K-$30K in phantom profit. See the [full formula broken down below](#the-formula-most-flippers-use-and-why-its-wrong).

You bought a house for $120K, put $40K into the rehab, and sold it for $210K. Quick math says you made $50K. That's the number you tell people at the REIA meeting.

The actual number, after closing costs on both sides, five months of hard money interest, insurance, utilities, property taxes, the dumpster rental you forgot about, and the agent's commission? Closer to $28K. Maybe less.

The gap between "what you think you made" and "what you actually made" is where flippers get into trouble. This article gives you the complete formula so your deal analysis reflects reality, not bar napkin math.

---

## The Formula Most Flippers Use (And Why It's Wrong)

**The bar napkin formula:**
> Sale Price - Purchase Price - Rehab = Profit

This leaves out roughly 8-15% of the deal's actual costs. On a $200K sale, that's $16K to $30K in costs that never made it into the calculation.

**The real formula:**
> Sale Price - (Acquisition Costs + Rehab Costs + Holding Costs + Selling Costs) = True Net Profit

Every line item in that formula has sub-items. Let's break them down.

---

## Acquisition Costs (What It Actually Cost to Buy)

**The purchase price is just the starting point.** Add:

- **Purchase price** — the contract number
- **Closing costs** — title insurance, title search, recording fees, transfer taxes
- **Origination points** — if you used [hard money](/blog/hard-money-loan-accounting-quickbooks), those 2 points on a $180K loan are $3,600 right there
- **Inspection and due diligence** — home inspection, environmental, survey
- **Attorney fees** — if your state requires an attorney at closing

On a typical NEPA flip, acquisition costs run 3-5% above the purchase price. On a $120K purchase, that's $3,600 to $6,000 you need to account for.

---

## Rehab Costs (The Obvious Ones and the Ones You Forget)

**Your contractor's invoice is the big number, but it's not the only number.** Rehab costs include everything you spent to get the house ready to sell:

- **General contractor or trade subs** — the main rehab bill
- **Materials you purchased directly** — every Home Depot and supply house run
- **Permits and inspections** — building permits, electrical permits, plumbing permits
- **Dumpster rentals and hauling** — demo debris doesn't disappear for free
- **Architectural or engineering** — if you needed plans drawn or a structural assessment
- **Staging** — furniture rental, decor
- **Cleaning** — post-construction cleaning, window washing

We track all of this by trade category in QuickBooks — see our guide on [tracking rehab costs in QBO](/blog/track-rehab-costs-quickbooks) for the exact setup.

Typical miss: the small stuff. A $47 trip to the hardware store doesn't seem worth tracking. Do that 30 times over a 3-month rehab and you've got $1,400 that never made it into your profit calculation.

---

## Holding Costs (The Silent Margin Killer)

Every month the deal is open, you're bleeding money. We wrote a whole article on [holding costs](/blog/holding-costs-house-flip) because they're that important. The short version:

- **Hard money interest** — 10-14% annual, paid monthly. At 12% on $180K, that's $1,800/month.
- **Property taxes** — prorated for the hold period
- **Insurance** — builder's risk or vacant property policy, usually $150-300/month
- **Utilities** — electric, gas, water during rehab. Contractors need lights and running water.
- **HOA fees** — if applicable

On a 5-month hold with hard money, holding costs can easily hit $12K-15K. That's money that comes straight off your profit.

The longer the deal sits, the more it costs. A flip that takes 3 months and a flip that takes 7 months have the same rehab cost — but very different holding costs.

---

## Selling Costs (The Exit Tax)

Selling a house isn't free either:

- **Agent commission** — typically 5-6% of sale price. On $210K, that's $10,500 to $12,600.
- **Seller-paid closing costs** — transfer taxes, title charges, recording fees
- **Buyer concessions** — if you agreed to cover buyer's closing costs
- **Home warranty** — if offered as part of the deal
- **Photography and marketing** — listing photos, drone shots, virtual tours

Agent commission alone is the single biggest line item most flippers underestimate. At 5.5% on a $210K sale, you're handing over $11,550. That $50K "profit" is now $38,450 before anything else.

## The Full Calculation: A Real Example

Let's run the numbers on a real deal:

| Category | Line Item | Amount |

| **Purchase** | Purchase price | $120,000 |
| | Closing costs | $3,200 |
| | Hard money points (2 pts) | $3,600 |
| **Rehab** | Contractor + materials | $38,500 |

| | Utilities | $950 |
| **Selling** | Agent commission (5.5%) | $11,550 |

The bar napkin said $50K. The real number is $10,850.

> **Key takeaway:** The bar napkin said $50K. The real number is $10,850. That's a real return — but it's a very different number than what most flippers would tell their friends.

That's not a bad deal — it's a 5.2% return in 5 months. But it's a very different number than $50K. And if you're making business decisions based on the $50K number, you're going to scale yourself into a cash flow problem.

## The Numbers You Should Track Per Deal

Every completed flip should have a one-page deal summary that shows:

- **Total acquisition cost** (purchase + closing + financing points)
- **Total rehab cost** (broken down by trade)
- **Total holding cost** (broken down by month)
- **Total selling cost** (commission + closing + concessions)
- **All-in cost** (everything above, added together)
- **Sale price**
- **Net profit**
- **ROI** (net profit / total cash invested)
- **Annualized ROI** (ROI adjusted for hold time)
- **Hold time** (days from closing to closing)

When you have this for every deal, you start seeing patterns. Which neighborhoods produce the best margins. Which contractors stay on budget. Which deal sources bring the most profitable projects.

That's how you go from "flipping houses" to "running a flipping business."
