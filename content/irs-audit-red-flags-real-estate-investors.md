# IRS Audit Red Flags for Real Estate Investors (and How Clean Books Protect You)

*Real estate investors show up disproportionately in IRS audit statistics. Not because they're doing anything wrong — but because messy books make it look like they might be.*

---

## Why Real Estate Investors Get Audited More

The IRS knows that real estate income is easy to underreport and real estate deductions are easy to overstate. Mix in LLCs, multiple properties, and a bookkeeper who doesn't understand REI — and you've got a return that practically invites scrutiny.

The good news: a clean audit is just paperwork. The bad news: a messy audit is expensive, stressful, and can result in penalties and back taxes that wipe out years of profits.

Here's what the IRS looks for — and what your books need to show.

---

## Red Flag #1: Repair Deductions That Are Too Large

Deducting a $40,000 "repair" expense on a property that you acquired for $85,000 will get attention. The IRS scrutinizes large repair deductions because repairs are immediately deductible while capital improvements must be depreciated over time.

**What auditors look for:** Repair deductions that are disproportionate to the property value, or that suspiciously coincide with a major renovation.

**What your books need to show:** Clear documentation separating repairs (deductible) from improvements (capitalizable) — with receipts, contractor invoices, and a clear explanation of the work performed. The IRS's Tangible Property Regulations are specific about this distinction.

---

## Red Flag #2: Rental Losses Against W-2 Income

The passive activity loss rules limit how much rental loss you can deduct against ordinary income — unless you qualify as a real estate professional. Claiming large rental losses against a high W-2 salary without documentation of real estate professional status is one of the most common audit triggers.

**What auditors look for:** Large Schedule E losses paired with significant W-2 income, without evidence of the hours required for real estate professional status.

**What your books need to show:** A time log documenting hours spent on real estate activities, property-level financials showing actual activity, and evidence that your real estate work represents your primary professional activity.

---

## Red Flag #3: Home Office and Vehicle Deductions

Mixed personal/business expenses — particularly home office and vehicle use — are chronically overstated by investors. The IRS sees this consistently.

**What auditors look for:** 100% business use of a vehicle for a part-time investor. Home office deductions without a dedicated, exclusively-used space.

**What your books need to show:** Mileage logs for vehicle use, documentation of the specific home office space, and clear separation between personal and business expenses in your QuickBooks file.

---

## Red Flag #4: Missing or Inconsistent 1099s

If you paid a contractor more than $600 in a calendar year and didn't issue a 1099-NEC, that's a problem. The IRS cross-references contractor payments, and discrepancies attract attention.

**What auditors look for:** Contractor deductions that don't have corresponding 1099s filed, or contractors whose reported income doesn't match your payments.

**What your books need to show:** Every vendor payment tracked in QuickBooks by contractor, with W-9s collected before the first payment and 1099s issued by January 31st. This is year-round bookkeeping work, not a January scramble.

---

## Red Flag #5: Inconsistent Income Reporting (Especially for Flippers)

House flippers often blur the lines between dealer income (ordinary income, self-employment tax) and investor income (capital gains). The IRS looks at deal frequency, holding periods, and the nature of activity to make this determination — and inconsistent reporting is a major red flag.

**What auditors look for:** Profits reported as capital gains when deal frequency, holding periods, or the use of LLCs suggests dealer status.

**What your books need to show:** Accurate per-project P&Ls showing acquisition date, holding period, improvement costs, and sale proceeds — with consistent classification across your return.

---

## Red Flag #6: Schedule E with No Activity or Inconsistent Activity

If you own rental properties but your Schedule E shows no income and no expenses, auditors wonder why. Rental properties generate transactions. Silence looks like omission.

**What auditors look for:** Properties listed on your return with no activity, or significant year-over-year swings in income or expenses without explanation.

**What your books need to show:** Active, accurate monthly bookkeeping for every property — even months when nothing unusual happened. A clean QuickBooks ledger shows continuous, explainable activity.

---

## What Surviving an Audit Actually Requires

The investors who sail through audits have one thing in common: their books are organized, property-level, and explainable.

That means:
- Every expense tied to a specific property or project
- Every deduction backed by a document (invoice, receipt, contract)
- Every contractor payment tracked and 1099-filed
- Every repair/improvement clearly distinguished and documented
- A QuickBooks file that tells the same story as your tax return

You don't need to be afraid of the IRS. You need clean books.
