# Per-Property P&L: How to Know What Each Door Is Actually Making You

*You think your portfolio is profitable. But which doors are actually pulling their weight?*

---

Here's a question I ask every new client: Can you pull up a profit and loss statement for 427 Oak Street right now?

Not your whole portfolio. Not your LLC. That one property. Income, expenses, mortgage allocation, vacancy, maintenance — all of it, for that address specifically.

Nine times out of ten, they can't.

And that's the problem.

---

## Why It Matters

You might be thinking: "I know roughly what each property makes. I collect the rent, I pay the bills."

That's not knowing your numbers. That's guessing.

Here's what "guessing" actually costs you:

**You're holding losers without knowing it.** I've seen investors with 8, 10, 12 properties where two of them are quietly destroying the portfolio's overall returns. But because everything gets lumped together, the profitable doors are subsidizing the bad ones — and nobody knows it.

**You're making growth decisions on bad data.** When you're deciding whether to buy your next property, refinance, or sell, you're using your instincts instead of actual per-property performance data. That's expensive.

**You can't get lender-ready financials.** Any serious lender — especially for a DSCR loan — wants to see property-level income and expense documentation. If your books are portfolio-wide, you're either scrambling to recreate it or losing deals.

**Your CPA is working with incomplete information.** Tax strategy for real estate investors is property-specific. Cost segregation, depreciation schedules, 1031 exchange timing — none of it works right without property-level books.

---

## What Per-Property Tracking Actually Looks Like

In QuickBooks, per-property tracking is built around **classes** or **locations** (depending on which QBO plan you're on). Every transaction gets tagged to a specific property.

That means:
- Rent collected in March for 427 Oak → tagged to 427 Oak
- Plumber's invoice from April → tagged to 427 Oak
- Mortgage principal + interest split → allocated to 427 Oak
- Insurance proration → allocated to 427 Oak

Then at any point, you can run a Profit & Loss by Class/Location and see exactly what each door did — this month, this quarter, this year, last year.

It sounds straightforward. But getting it set up correctly from the beginning, and maintaining it consistently, is where most landlords fall apart. Especially as the portfolio grows.

---

## The Income Side

Every property should track:

**Rental income** by unit (if multi-family). A duplex at 427 Oak should show Unit A income and Unit B income separately, even if they're both part of the same property's P&L.

**Late fees.** Small, but they add up and they're worth tracking.

**Laundry or parking income** if applicable.

**Security deposit accounting.** This is its own topic — deposits shouldn't be income until they're actually applied. A lot of DIY books get this wrong.

---

## The Expense Side

This is where it gets detailed. Per-property expenses should include:

**Direct property costs:**
- Repairs and maintenance
- Utilities (if landlord-paid)
- Property management fees (if applicable)
- Landscaping and snow removal
- Trash removal
- Pest control

**Mortgage allocation:**
- This is critical and often missed. The full mortgage payment isn't an expense — only the *interest* portion is deductible. Principal is a balance sheet item. You need to split the payment correctly every month.

**Property taxes.** Usually paid through escrow, which makes tracking more complex — you're tracking an escrow account, not just a direct payment.

**Insurance.** Same issue as taxes — often escrowed, needs to be allocated properly.

**[Capital expenditures](/blog/capex-vs-repairs).** Major improvements get capitalized and depreciated on their own depreciation schedule, tracked at the property level. Getting this classification right matters — a lot.

## Multi-Entity Complications

If you hold properties in multiple LLCs — which you probably should for asset protection — per-property tracking has an additional layer. We cover this in depth in our guide to [multi-entity LLC bookkeeping](/blog/multi-entity-llc-bookkeeping).

Each LLC needs its own QuickBooks file (or at minimum its own company within QBO). Intercompany transfers between entities need to be recorded as loans or equity contributions, not just expenses. And you need a way to see your whole portfolio's performance across all entities, not just each one in isolation.

This is one of the trickier bookkeeping setups to do right. But when it works, you have a complete picture: property-level performance within each entity, plus portfolio-wide reporting across all your LLCs.

---

## The Reporting Cadence

Once your books are set up correctly, per-property P&Ls should be a monthly thing — not just year-end. Here's what we send our clients every month:

- **Portfolio summary:** Top-line numbers across all properties
- **Per-property P&L:** Income, expenses, and net operating income by address
- **Cash flow summary:** What actually hit the bank accounts
- **Exception report:** Any property that performed significantly above or below expectations

When you get used to seeing these every month, you stop guessing. You know which doors are your workhorses, which ones need attention, and which ones might be candidates to sell.
