# Real Estate Dealer vs. Investor: Why the IRS Classification Matters for Flippers

You flip houses. You buy low, rehab, sell high. You do this regularly — maybe 4 or 5 deals a year, maybe more. You file your taxes and your CPA reports the income as capital gains.

Then one day the IRS decides you're not an "investor." You're a "dealer." And now you owe self-employment tax on every dollar of flip profit you've ever reported as capital gains.

This isn't a hypothetical. It happens. And the difference between dealer and investor status can mean tens of thousands of dollars in additional taxes. Your books are one of the key factors the IRS looks at when making this determination.

After reading this, you'll understand what the classification means, how the IRS decides, and what your bookkeeping needs to show to protect you.

> **The short version:** The IRS classifies frequent flippers as "dealers" — which means ordinary income tax plus 15.3% self-employment tax on every dollar of profit. No capital gains treatment. No 1031 exchanges. The classification depends on frequency, intent, and holding period. Your books and entity structure are key factors — clean [deal-level P&Ls](/blog/fix-and-flip-accounting) and separate entities for flips vs. rentals protect you.

---

## Investor vs. Dealer: The Short Version

**Investor:** You buy and hold properties for appreciation, rental income, or both. When you sell, the profit is a capital gain. If you held for more than a year, it's a long-term capital gain — taxed at favorable rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income).

**Dealer:** You buy properties with the primary intent of reselling them for profit. The properties are "inventory," not investments. Profit is ordinary income, subject to:
- Regular income tax rates (up to 37%)
- Self-employment tax (15.3% on the first ~$160K, 2.9% above that)
- No eligibility for 1031 exchanges
- No long-term capital gains treatment

On a $50K flip profit, the difference between investor and dealer classification could be $7,500 to $12,000 in additional taxes. Per deal.

## How the IRS Decides

**There's no single rule — the IRS uses a multi-factor test,** and courts have upheld various combinations of these factors over the years.

### Frequency and holding period

**Frequency and volume of sales.** If you flip 10 houses a year, that looks like a business. If you sell one rental property you've held for 8 years, that looks like an investment.

**How long you hold the property.** Buying, rehabbing, and selling within 4 months screams dealer. Holding a rental for 5 years and then selling looks like an investor.

### Intent and income mix

**Intent at the time of purchase.** Did you buy it to flip, or did you buy it to hold? The IRS looks at your actions, not just your words. If you bought it, immediately started a rehab, and listed it 3 months later — the intent is clear.

**How much of your income comes from sales.** If flip profits are your primary income source, the IRS is more likely to classify you as a dealer.

### Business activity and improvements

**Whether you're in the "business" of selling real estate.** Do you have a business entity for flipping? Do you market yourself as a flipper? Do you have a website, business cards, or marketing spend focused on acquiring properties to resell? All of these point toward dealer status.

**Improvements made to the property.** Extensive improvements before resale suggest dealer activity. A buy-and-hold investor who sells "as-is" looks more like an investor.

No single factor is determinative. The IRS looks at the totality — and it's a judgment call, which means gray areas are common.

> **Key takeaway:** The IRS doesn't use a bright-line test. They look at the full picture — how many deals you do, how long you hold, how you market yourself, and what your books say. Gray areas are common, which is exactly why your documentation matters.

## Why Your Books Matter

Here's where bookkeeping becomes a tax strategy issue, not just an accounting issue.

**Clean books show intent.** If you have separate entities — one for flips, one for holds — and your books clearly distinguish between properties held as inventory (flips) and properties held as investments (rentals), you're demonstrating to the IRS that you understand the difference and have organized your business accordingly.

**Messy books invite scrutiny.** If every property — flips and rentals — runs through the same entity, the same bank account, and the same QuickBooks file with no distinction, the IRS has an easier argument that it's all one business activity: dealing.

**Deal-level P&Ls support your position.** When each flip has its own [project-level P&L](/blog/fix-and-flip-accounting) showing acquisition costs, rehab tracking, holding costs, and sale proceeds, your books tell a coherent story. They show that each deal was managed as a discrete project — which is how dealers work, yes, but also how professionals organize their books regardless of classification.

**Entity structure matters.** Many flippers use a separate LLC for their flipping activity, distinct from their rental holdings. This creates a clear paper trail: Flip LLC is the dealer entity, Rental LLC is the investor entity. Your bookkeeping needs to keep these completely separate — [separate books, separate bank accounts](/blog/separate-bank-accounts-rental-property), separate P&Ls.

## Common Strategies (Talk to Your CPA)

I'm a bookkeeper, not a CPA or tax attorney. This isn't tax advice. But here are the strategies I see our clients' CPAs implement regularly:

**Dual entity structure.** One LLC for flips (treated as dealer), one LLC for rentals (treated as investor). The flip LLC pays self-employment tax on its profits. The rental LLC enjoys capital gains treatment. The books for each entity are completely separate.

**S-Corp election for the flip entity.** Some flippers elect S-Corp status for their flipping LLC. Profits pass through to the owner, but only "reasonable compensation" (W-2 salary) is subject to self-employment tax. The remaining profit is distributed without SE tax. This can save thousands annually — but the salary has to be defensible.

**Hold some properties long-term.** If you buy a property intending to flip it, but the market shifts and you decide to rent it instead, the holding period and rental income help support investor classification for that property. But you can't retroactively change your intent — the IRS looks at what you actually did.

**Document everything.** For borderline situations, documentation is your defense. Board meeting minutes, written investment plans, loan applications stating "rental property" vs. "fix-and-flip" — these all create a paper trail of intent.

## What Your Bookkeeper Should Be Doing

Regardless of how you and your CPA handle the tax classification, your bookkeeping should:

1. **Separate flip activity from rental activity** — different entities, different QBO files, different bank accounts
2. **Track each flip as a project** with a complete [deal P&L](/blog/fix-and-flip-accounting)
3. **Classify expenses correctly** — rehab costs as cost of goods sold (for dealer activity), not operating expenses
4. **Track [holding costs](/blog/holding-costs-house-flip) per deal** — interest, taxes, insurance, utilities
5. **Maintain [1099 records](/blog/1099-for-contractors-landlords) for all contractors** — this is non-negotiable for any entity doing significant construction work
6. **Keep records that support your CPA's tax position** — whatever strategy they recommend, the books need to back it up

> **Key takeaway:** Your books need to tell a story that supports your CPA's tax position. Separate entities for flips vs. rentals, [separate bank accounts](/blog/separate-bank-accounts-rental-property), and clean deal-level P&Ls per flip — that's the foundation.

The worst position to be in is having your CPA take an aggressive tax position (investor treatment for flip income) while your books tell a different story (no entity separation, everything lumped together). That's how audits turn into penalties.
