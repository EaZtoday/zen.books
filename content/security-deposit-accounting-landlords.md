# Security Deposit Accounting for Landlords: How to Record It Without Messing Up Your Books

You collect a security deposit from a new tenant and drop it into your checking account. QuickBooks sees the deposit. You see the deposit. And now your P&L says you made more money this month than you actually did.

That's the mistake. Security deposits aren't income. They're money you're holding on behalf of the tenant. And if your books treat them as income, your [per-property P&L](/blog/per-property-pl-tracking) is wrong, your tax picture is wrong, and you're going to have a bad time when the tenant moves out and wants their money back.

After reading this, you'll know exactly how to record security deposits in QuickBooks so they show up in the right place from day one.

---

## Why Security Deposits Aren't Income

This trips up a lot of landlords. Money hits the bank account, so it feels like income. But a security deposit is a liability — you owe it back to the tenant unless you have a legitimate reason to keep it.

Think of it like this: if someone hands you $1,500 and says "hold this for me," you didn't earn $1,500. You're just the custodian. The deposit sits on your balance sheet as a liability, not on your P&L as revenue.

The only time a security deposit becomes income is when you're legally entitled to keep it — typically for unpaid rent or damages beyond normal wear and tear, per your state's landlord-tenant law.

---

## How to Record a Security Deposit in QuickBooks

Here's the setup. It takes five minutes and saves you from a mess later.

**Step 1: Create a liability account.**

In your Chart of Accounts, create a new account:
- Type: Other Current Liabilities
- Name: "Tenant Security Deposits" (or "Security Deposits Held")

This is where all deposits live until they're refunded or applied.

**Step 2: Record the deposit when received.**

When a tenant pays a security deposit, record it as a deposit into your bank account, but categorize it to the "Tenant Security Deposits" liability account — not to rental income.

If you want to track which tenant's deposit is which (you should), add the tenant name or property address in the memo field. Some landlords create sub-accounts per property.

**Step 3: That's it — until move-out.**

The deposit sits on your balance sheet. It doesn't touch your P&L. Your income numbers stay accurate. Your property-level reporting stays clean.

---

## What Happens at Move-Out

This is where it gets interesting. Three scenarios:

**Scenario 1: Full refund.**

Tenant moves out, unit is in good shape, you send back the full deposit. Record a check or bank transfer from your bank account, categorized to the "Tenant Security Deposits" liability account. The liability goes to zero. No income or expense is recorded. Simple.

**Scenario 2: Partial refund with deductions.**

Tenant left a hole in the wall and didn't pay the last month's water bill. You deduct $400 for damages and $85 for the utility bill.

Split the transaction:
- $1,015 to the liability account (the refund)
- $400 to a "Damage Income" or "Security Deposit Forfeit" revenue account
- $85 to the appropriate utility expense recovery account

The $400 and $85 are now properly recorded as income in the month you applied them. Not when you collected the deposit a year ago.

**Scenario 3: Full forfeiture.**

Tenant trashed the place and skipped out. You're keeping the entire deposit (within what your state law allows). Record a journal entry: debit the liability account, credit a revenue account. The full amount is now income in the current period.

## The State Law Problem

Every state has different rules about security deposits. Some require you to hold them in a separate bank account. Some require an interest-bearing account. Some have specific timelines for returning the deposit after move-out (Pennsylvania gives you 30 days).

Your books need to reflect whatever your state requires. If your state says deposits must be in a separate account, you need a separate bank account in QBO connected to that liability account.

This isn't optional. It's the law. And if you end up in a dispute with a tenant, your books are your evidence that you handled the deposit correctly.

## Common Mistakes We See

**Recording deposits as rental income.** The most common one. Your P&L is inflated, and when you refund the deposit later, you have a mysterious "expense" that doesn't make sense.

**Not tracking deposits by tenant or property.** When you manage 10+ units, you need to know exactly whose deposit you're holding and how much. A single lump-sum liability account with no detail is a guessing game at move-out. Use the memo field, sub-accounts, or a simple tracking spreadsheet alongside QBO.

**Forgetting about deposits when selling a property.** If you sell a rental that has active tenants, those security deposits transfer to the new owner. Your books need to reflect the transfer — the liability moves off your balance sheet as part of the sale. Miss this and your books will show phantom liabilities for tenants you no longer have.

**Mixing deposits with operating funds.** Even if your state doesn't legally require a separate deposit account, it's good practice. Co-mingling deposits with operating cash makes it easy to accidentally spend money you owe someone. That's how landlords end up scrambling for cash when three tenants move out in the same month.

## How This Fits Into Your Overall Books

Security deposit accounting is one piece of a bigger picture. When your deposits are tracked correctly:

- Your [per-property P&L](/blog/per-property-pl-tracking) reflects actual earned income, not inflated numbers from collected deposits
- Your balance sheet accurately shows how much you're holding for tenants
- Move-out accounting is clean — you know exactly what was collected, what's being returned, and what's being kept
- If you have properties in [multiple LLCs](/blog/multi-entity-llc-bookkeeping), each entity's deposits stay separated

It's one of those things that takes 5 minutes to set up right and saves hours of cleanup later.
