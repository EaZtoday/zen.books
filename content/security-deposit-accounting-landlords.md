# Security Deposit Accounting for Landlords

*Security deposits aren't income. Here's how to record them so your books stay clean.*

---

> **The short version:** A security deposit is money you're holding for the tenant — not money you earned. Record it as a liability in QuickBooks, not as rental income. When the tenant moves out, it either gets refunded (liability goes to zero) or applied to damages (then it becomes income). Get this wrong and your [per-property P&L](/blog/per-property-pl-tracking) is inflated from day one.

---

## Why Security Deposits Aren't Income

You collect a deposit from a new tenant and drop it into your checking account. QuickBooks sees the deposit. You see the deposit. And now your P&L says you made more money this month than you actually did.

**That's the mistake.** A security deposit is a liability — you owe it back to the tenant unless you have a legitimate reason to keep it.

Think of it like this: if someone hands you $1,500 and says "hold this for me," you didn't earn $1,500. You're just the custodian.

The deposit sits on your balance sheet as a liability, not on your P&L as revenue. The only time it becomes income is when you're legally entitled to keep it — typically for unpaid rent or damages beyond normal wear and tear.

---

## How to Record a Security Deposit in QuickBooks

The setup takes five minutes and saves you from a mess later.

**Step 1: Create a liability account.**

In your Chart of Accounts, create a new account:
- Type: Other Current Liabilities
- Name: "Tenant Security Deposits"

This is where all deposits live until they're refunded or applied.

**Step 2: Record the deposit when received.**

When a tenant pays, record the deposit into your bank account — but categorize it to the liability account, **not** to rental income.

Add the tenant name or property address in the memo field. Some landlords create sub-accounts per property to keep things tidy.

**Step 3: That's it — until move-out.**

The deposit sits on your balance sheet. It doesn't touch your P&L. Your income numbers stay accurate. Your property-level reporting stays clean.

---

## What Happens at Move-Out

This is where it gets interesting. Three scenarios:

### Full Refund

Tenant moves out, unit is in good shape, you send back the full deposit.

Record a check or bank transfer from your bank account, categorized to the liability account. The liability goes to zero. No income or expense recorded.

### Partial Refund

Tenant left a hole in the wall and didn't pay the last water bill. You deduct $400 for damages and $85 for utilities.

**Split the transaction:**
- $1,015 → liability account (the refund)
- $400 → "Damage Income" revenue account
- $85 → utility expense recovery account

The $400 and $85 are now income **in the month you applied them** — not when you collected the deposit a year ago.

### Full Forfeiture

Tenant trashed the place and skipped out. You're keeping the entire deposit (within what your state allows).

Record a journal entry: debit the liability account, credit a revenue account. The full amount is now income in the current period.

## The State Law Problem

> **Pennsylvania gives landlords 30 days** to return a security deposit after move-out. Some states require a separate bank account. Some require interest-bearing accounts. Your books need to reflect whatever your state requires — this isn't optional, it's the law.

If your state says deposits must be held in a separate account, you need a separate bank account in QBO connected to that liability account.

If you end up in a dispute with a tenant, your books are your evidence that you handled the deposit correctly.

## Common Mistakes We See

**Recording deposits as rental income.** The most common one. Your P&L is inflated, and when you refund the deposit later, you have a mysterious "expense" that doesn't make sense.

**Not tracking deposits by tenant or property.** When you manage 10+ units, you need to know exactly whose deposit you're holding and how much. A single lump-sum liability with no detail is a guessing game at move-out.

**Forgetting about deposits when selling a property.** If you sell a rental with active tenants, those deposits transfer to the new owner. Your books need to reflect that transfer — the liability moves off your balance sheet as part of the sale.

**Mixing deposits with operating funds.** Even if your state doesn't legally require a separate deposit account, co-mingling deposits with operating cash makes it easy to accidentally spend money you owe someone. That's how landlords end up scrambling for cash when three tenants move out the same month.

## How This Fits Into Your Overall Books

When your deposits are tracked correctly:

- Your [per-property P&L](/blog/per-property-pl-tracking) reflects actual earned income — not inflated numbers from collected deposits
- Your balance sheet accurately shows how much you're holding for tenants
- Move-out accounting is clean — you know exactly what was collected, what's being returned, and what's being kept
- If you have properties in [multiple LLCs](/blog/multi-entity-llc-bookkeeping), each entity's deposits stay separated

> **The takeaway:** Security deposit accounting takes 5 minutes to set up right and saves hours of cleanup later. If you've been recording deposits as income, the fix is a reclassification — move them to the liability account, adjust your historical P&Ls, and move forward with the correct setup.
