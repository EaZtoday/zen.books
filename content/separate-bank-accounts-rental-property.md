# Do I Need a Separate Bank Account for Each Rental Property?

*Short answer: yes. Here's the longer answer — and what happens when you don't.*

---

> **The short version:** Every LLC needs its own bank account — that's non-negotiable. Ideally, you want one per property. Commingling funds weakens your liability protection, makes bookkeeping a nightmare, and costs you money at tax time. Here's how to set it up right and connect it to your [per-property P&L tracking](/blog/per-property-pl-tracking).

I get this question at least once a week. Usually from a landlord who's running three, four, five properties through a single checking account — sometimes their personal one — and wondering why their books are a mess every February.

The answer is yes, you need separate bank accounts. At minimum one per LLC. Ideally one per property. Let me explain why, and then I'll show you how to set it up without losing your mind.

---

## The Short Answer

**If you own rental properties in LLCs — and you should — each LLC needs its own dedicated bank account.** That's the floor. Not the ceiling.

Three reasons this matters:

### Liability Protection

The whole point of putting a property in an LLC is to keep it legally separate from your other assets. But if you're running LLC funds through your personal checking account — or mixing funds between two different LLCs — you're commingling.

Commingling is the fastest way for a plaintiff's attorney to pierce your corporate veil. If you're managing [multiple LLCs](/blog/multi-entity-llc-bookkeeping), commingling funds is the fastest way to lose the liability protection you set up in the first place.

### Bookkeeping Clarity

When each property (or at minimum each LLC) has its own account, reconciliation is straightforward. Rent comes in, expenses go out, and you can see exactly what happened for that property without filtering through hundreds of unrelated transactions.

### Lender Requirements

If you're applying for a DSCR loan or refinancing, the lender wants to see that your property operates as a standalone business. Separate bank statements make this easy. Mixed-use personal accounts make it nearly impossible.

> **Key takeaway:** Separate bank accounts aren't just a nice-to-have. They protect your LLCs, simplify your bookkeeping, and give lenders what they need to approve your next deal.

## What Happens When You Don't

**Here's a real example of what commingled accounts cost a landlord.**

I had a client come to us with six rental properties, all running through one personal Bank of America checking account. No LLCs at first — just everything in their name.

Tax season was a nightmare every year. Their CPA was guessing which deposits were rent and which were personal income. Expenses were mixed in with grocery runs and Amazon orders.

They were missing deductions left and right because nobody could tell which expenses belonged to which property. We spent three weeks sorting through two years of bank statements just to get them current.

That cleanup cost more than a full year of proper bookkeeping would have. Don't be that landlord.

## How to Set It Up in QuickBooks

**Once you have your bank accounts open — one per LLC at minimum — here's how to connect them in QuickBooks Online.**

**Step 1:** Open each bank account and connect it to QBO via bank feeds. Each account shows up as its own register.

**Step 2:** Set up Classes or Locations in QBO to tag transactions by property. Even if an LLC holds two properties with one bank account, you can still track at the property level using class tags.

**Step 3:** Create matching income and expense categories for each property type — rental income, repairs, property taxes, insurance, utilities.

**Step 4:** Reconcile monthly. Every single month. This is non-negotiable. If you skip reconciliation, you're back to guessing.

This is the foundation of [per-property P&L tracking](/blog/per-property-pl-tracking) — without separate accounts, you're making reconciliation ten times harder than it needs to be.

## How Many Accounts Is Too Many?

**There's no such thing as too many accounts — but there is such a thing as too complicated to manage without a system.**

Here's my rule of thumb:

- **One bank account per LLC** is the absolute minimum
- **One bank account per property** is ideal if you have fewer than 10 doors
- **One operating account per LLC plus one shared credit card** works if the credit card transactions are tagged properly per property
- **A separate savings account for reserves** is smart — put 5-10% of rent aside for CapEx and vacancy

The key isn't the number of accounts. It's that every dollar is traceable to the right property and the right entity.

## The Buy-and-Hold Investor Setup

**Here's what I set up for a typical client with 5-10 doors across two or three LLCs.**

- **LLC A operating checking** — receives rent, pays expenses for Properties 1-3
- **LLC B operating checking** — receives rent, pays expenses for Properties 4-6
- **Holding company checking** — receives distributions from LLCs A and B, pays entity-level expenses (insurance, legal, professional fees)
- **One business credit card** — used for materials and supplies across all properties, tagged per property in QuickBooks
- **Reserve savings account** — capital reserves held at the holding company level

Clean. Simple. Every dollar traceable.

This is exactly what we set up for [buy-and-hold clients](/buy-and-hold) during onboarding. It takes about a week to get right, and then your books practically run themselves every month.

> **The takeaway:** The goal isn't to have the fewest accounts possible. It's to make every dollar traceable to the right property and the right entity — without spending hours sorting through transactions.
