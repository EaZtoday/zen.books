# Stessa vs. QuickBooks for Fix-and-Flip: Why Most Flippers Use the Wrong Tool

*The software debate nobody has honestly answered for flippers.*

---

> **The short version:** Stessa was built for rental portfolios, not flips. QuickBooks was built for ongoing businesses, not project-based rehabs. If you're using either one out of the box for fix-and-flip without understanding the limitations, you're almost certainly producing books that frustrate your CPA and leave money on the table. Here's the honest breakdown — and what actually works.

I talk to a lot of flippers who picked their bookkeeping software the same way most people pick a contractor: someone in a Facebook group mentioned it, so they went with it.

Stessa comes up constantly. "It's free!" "It's made for real estate!" "Everybody uses it!"

QuickBooks comes up too. "It's what my accountant uses." "It does everything." "It connects to my bank."

Both statements are true. Neither means the software is right for your business.

---

## The Fundamental Problem: Flips Are Projects, Not Properties

Here's what Stessa and standard QuickBooks both struggle with when it comes to fix-and-flip:

**Your flip is a project with a beginning, middle, and end.** You buy a property, spend money improving it, and sell it. The whole purpose of your books is to capture:
- What you paid for it (acquisition cost)
- Everything you spent improving it (cost basis additions)
- What you sold it for (gross proceeds)
- Your actual profit per deal

That's job costing. Project-based accounting. Neither Stessa nor a default QuickBooks setup does this well without significant customization.

---

## Stessa: Built for Buy-and-Hold, Not Flips

Stessa is genuinely excellent software — for rental property owners. It's designed around the idea of a property that generates monthly income and ongoing expenses. That model works beautifully for landlords.

For flippers, the cracks show fast:

**Problem 1: No project-based cost tracking.** Stessa tracks expenses against properties. But for a flip, you need to track expenses as they build toward your cost basis — each draw, each subcontractor invoice, each permit fee. Stessa doesn't distinguish between an ongoing maintenance expense (deductible now) and a capital improvement (added to cost basis). That distinction is everything for a flipper.

**Problem 2: No inventory accounting.** When you're a dealer — doing multiple flips per year — the IRS may classify your properties as inventory, not capital assets. Stessa doesn't handle inventory accounting at all. This matters enormously for how you report profit and what self-employment taxes you owe.

**Problem 3: Limited job costing.** If you're running two or three simultaneous flips, Stessa can't give you a clean picture of what each project has spent, what's left in the budget, or what the projected margin looks like.

> **Bottom line on Stessa:** If you own rental properties alongside your flipping operation, Stessa is excellent for the rental side. Don't use it as your primary flip accounting tool.

---

## QuickBooks: Powerful but Not Plug-and-Play for Flippers

QuickBooks Online is capable of handling fix-and-flip accounting well — but not without deliberate setup. Out of the box, it's configured for a standard business that buys things and sells services or goods. That setup creates problems for flippers.

**Problem 1: Default chart of accounts doesn't match flip accounting.** The standard setup has "Cost of Goods Sold" and "Inventory" accounts, but without a dedicated flipper setup, you'll end up with rehab costs in the wrong places — sometimes expensed when they should be capitalized, sometimes treated as inventory when they should be on the balance sheet.

**Problem 2: No built-in job costing out of standard settings.** QuickBooks Online Plus and Advanced have job costing features (Projects). The basic plan doesn't. If you're on Simple Start or Essentials, you're tracking rehab costs with workarounds.

**Problem 3: Generic reports don't show per-flip profitability.** The default P&L is your whole business. To see per-flip margins, you need to use classes or projects correctly — and most people don't set this up right from the start.

> **Bottom line on QuickBooks:** With the right setup — correct chart of accounts, Projects turned on, proper class tracking — QuickBooks Online Plus handles flip accounting well. ## What Actually Works: QuickBooks Set Up Specifically for Flips

When I set up books for a fix-and-flip client, here's what the structure looks like:

**Chart of accounts built for flips:**
- A separate asset account for each active flip property (tracks your running investment/basis)
- COGS accounts that activate when the property sells, not when costs are incurred
- Liability accounts for hard money draws (correctly recording the loan, not just the cash)
- Income account that records the full sale proceeds, not just "profit"

**Projects (job costing) turned on:**
- Every transaction tagged to the specific flip
- Budget vs. actual visible per deal
- Margin visible before closing

**Class tracking for multi-entity operations:**
- If you're flipping across multiple LLCs, class tracking keeps the books clean without needing separate QuickBooks subscriptions for each entity

**What this gives you:**
- A clear per-deal P&L that actually matches your settlement statement
- A cost basis number your CPA can use directly
- Draw-by-draw loan tracking that reconciles to your hard money lender's statement
- The data your accountant needs to determine dealer vs. investor classification

## The Honest Recommendation

| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| 1-3 flips/year, also own rentals | QuickBooks Online Plus (flips) + Stessa (rentals) |
| 4+ flips/year, no rentals | QuickBooks Online Plus, set up specifically for flips |
| Just starting, testing the waters | QuickBooks Simple Start with a clean manual system |
| Multi-entity, 6+ deals/year | QuickBooks Online Plus with class tracking, or Advanced |
| Flips + a real estate business entity | QuickBooks with proper entity separation — [see multi-entity setup](/blog/multi-entity-llc-bookkeeping) |

## The Bigger Problem: It's Not Just the Software

Honestly, the software matters less than the setup and the person running it.

A general bookkeeper who doesn't understand fix-and-flip accounting will produce bad numbers regardless of what platform they're on. Stessa with a knowledgeable person beats QuickBooks with someone who doesn't understand cost basis.

What you actually need is someone who understands:
- When rehab costs become part of your basis vs. when they're expensed
- How to record hard money draws and payments correctly
- How to classify you as a dealer vs. investor based on your activity
- How to structure the books so your CPA gets clean data, not a spreadsheet to reverse-engineer
