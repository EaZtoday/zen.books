# How to Track Rehab Costs for a Flip in QuickBooks

*Your rehab budget says $45K. You've spent... something. If your books can't tell you the exact number right now, we need to fix your setup.*

## Why General Categories Don't Work for Flips

**The default QuickBooks chart of accounts has categories like "Repairs & Maintenance," "Materials," and "Contractor Payments."** A lot of flippers dump everything into one or two of these buckets.

That tells you almost nothing useful. You can't compare actual costs to your budget by trade. You can't figure out where the overruns happened.

You can't build better estimates for your next project because all the detail is gone. Flips need project-level tracking with trade-level detail.

QuickBooks can do this — but you have to set it up intentionally.

---

## The QuickBooks Setup (Step by Step)

**Here's how to structure it.** I'm using QuickBooks Online Plus or Advanced — you need the Projects feature or Classes to make this work properly.

### Step 1: Create a Project for Each Flip

In QBO, go to Projects and create a new project. Name it by address — "123 Main St" or whatever your naming convention is. Every transaction related to this flip gets assigned to this project.

### Step 2: Set Up Your Chart of Accounts

Under Cost of Goods Sold (or a "Rehab Costs" parent account), create sub-accounts for each trade category:

- Demo and hauling
- Framing and carpentry
- Plumbing
- Electrical
- HVAC
- Roofing
- Siding and exterior
- Flooring
- Drywall and paint
- Kitchen (cabinets, counters, appliances)
- Bathrooms
- Landscaping and curb appeal
- Permits and inspections
- Miscellaneous / punch list

### Step 3: Tag Every Expense to Both the Project and the Trade Category

When you enter a bill from your plumber, it goes to the "Plumbing" account and is assigned to the "123 Main St" project. When you buy flooring at the supply house, it goes to "Flooring" under the same project.

This gives you two views: total spend by project, and spend by trade within each project.

> **Key takeaway:** The dual-tagging is what makes this work. Every expense needs both a trade category and a project assignment. Skip either one and you lose the ability to compare budget-to-actual by trade on each deal.

## Tracking Contractor Draws and Materials

**There's a difference between a contractor draw and a materials purchase, and your books should reflect it.**

### Contractor Draws

Contractor draws are payments against a contract. If your GC has a $30K contract and submits a $10K draw request for completed work, that's a bill.

Enter it as a bill in QBO, assign it to the right trade category (or split it across categories if the draw covers multiple trades), and assign it to the project.

### Materials Purchases

Materials purchases are your direct buys — lumber from the supply house, fixtures from the plumbing wholesaler, that emergency run to Home Depot at 7am. These are expenses, coded to the appropriate trade category and tagged to the project.

Attach receipts to every transaction. QBO lets you snap a photo and attach it directly.

Do this in real time, not three months later when you can't remember what a $347 charge was for.

## Holding Costs Belong in the Project Too

**This is where a lot of flippers miss money.** Holding costs are real project costs — they eat into your profit just as much as the rehab does.

These should all be tracked inside the project:

- **Hard money interest** — every monthly payment during the hold period
- **Insurance** — builder's risk or vacant property policy
- **Property taxes** — prorated for the hold period
- **Utilities** — electric, water, gas during rehab
- **HOA fees** — if applicable

When you include holding costs in the project, your deal P&L tells the real story. That flip that "made $40K" might have only made $28K after five months of hard money payments and carrying costs.

> **The takeaway:** If holding costs aren't inside the project in QuickBooks, your deal P&L is overstating profit on every single flip. The $12K gap between what you think you made and what you actually made? That's where it hides.

## Budget vs. Actual

**Here's where it all pays off.**

Once your project is set up with trade-level categories, you can pull a Project Profitability report at any time and see exactly where you stand.

Set up your original budget as a note or spreadsheet alongside QBO. QuickBooks doesn't have great built-in budgeting for projects, so most flippers track the budget separately and compare against the QBO actuals.

### The Mid-Project Report

The report you want to see mid-project looks like this:

| Trade | Budget | Actual | Variance |
|-------|--------|--------|----------|
| Demo | $3,000 | $3,200 | -$200 |
| Plumbing | $5,000 | $4,800 | +$200 |
| Electrical | $4,000 | $6,100 | -$2,100 |
| ... | ... | ... | ... |
| **Total** | **$45,000** | **$38,500** | **-$2,100** |

When you see that electrical is $2,100 over budget halfway through the project, you can make decisions — cut scope somewhere else, renegotiate, or adjust your sale price expectations.

Without this data, you find out you're over budget after the flip sells.

## When the Flip Sells

**Closing day — here's how to record the sale in QuickBooks.**

**Step 1:** Record the sale price as income, assigned to the project. Use a "Flip Sale Revenue" or "Property Sale Proceeds" account.

**Step 2:** Record selling costs — agent commissions, seller-paid closing costs, transfer taxes, title fees. These are expenses within the project.

**Step 3:** Pull the final Project P&L. This is your deal summary:

- Sale price
- Minus: acquisition cost + closing costs
- Minus: total rehab (broken out by trade)
- Minus: holding costs
- Minus: selling costs
- **= Net profit on the deal**

That number is your actual profit. Not your estimated profit. Not your "I think we made about..." profit. The real number, backed by every receipt and invoice.
