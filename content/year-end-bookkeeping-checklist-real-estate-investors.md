# Year-End Bookkeeping Checklist for Real Estate Investors (Don't Hand Your CPA a Mess)

*Your CPA's hourly rate is not the place to sort out a year of disorganized expenses. Here's what you should have ready — and what it costs you not to.*

---

## Why Year-End Is So Painful for Real Estate Investors

Real estate bookkeeping is complex in ways that generic bookkeeping isn't. Multiple properties, multiple entities, mixed CapEx and repairs, contractor payments, loan accounting, and depreciation schedules — it compounds throughout the year. By December, investors who haven't kept up are staring at a mountain of bank statements, contractor invoices, and vague recollections.

CPAs charge hourly. When you hand them disorganized records, you're paying CPA rates for bookkeeper work. The investors who show up with clean books — organized by property, with every transaction categorized — get their returns filed faster, miss fewer deductions, and spend significantly less.

Here's what you need to have in order before year-end.

---

## The Checklist

### ✅ 1. Reconcile All Bank and Credit Card Accounts

Every account you used for real estate activity — business checking, credit cards, PayPal, Venmo — should be reconciled through December 31st. Every transaction should be categorized in QuickBooks Online. No unreviewed items, no uncategorized transactions.

**Why it matters:** Unreconciled accounts mean missed deductions and potential errors. Your CPA can't work from an unbalanced QuickBooks file.

---

### ✅ 2. Separate All Personal and Business Transactions

Go through every account and flag any personal transactions that hit a business account (or vice versa). These need to be properly classified — owner's draw, loan to owner, or clearly excluded. Commingled accounts are one of the top causes of missed deductions.

---

### ✅ 3. Finalize CapEx vs. Repair Classifications

Every improvement and repair made during the year should be classified as either a capitalizable improvement (added to your depreciation schedule) or a deductible repair. If you're not sure on any items, flag them for your CPA — but don't leave them uncategorized.

**Common gray areas:** New HVAC, roof replacement, kitchen renovations, electrical panel upgrades.

---

### ✅ 4. Collect W-9s and Prepare 1099-NECs

If you paid any contractor, vendor, or service provider more than $600 during the year, you're likely required to issue a 1099-NEC by January 31st. 

**Before year-end:**
- Confirm you have a W-9 on file for every vendor you paid $600+
- Run a vendor payment report in QuickBooks to identify anyone you may have missed
- Chase down any missing W-9s now — it's much harder to get them in January

---

### ✅ 5. Document All Loan Activity

Every mortgage draw, hard money loan disbursement, HELOC draw, and loan payoff should be categorized correctly in QuickBooks. Principal payments, interest payments, and loan fees are all different line items. Mixing these up overstates or understates your deductible interest expense.

---

### ✅ 6. Close Out Completed Flip Projects

For every flip you sold this year, make sure the project P&L is finalized:
- Acquisition cost
- All rehab expenses (by category)
- Holding costs (insurance, utilities, property taxes, interest)
- Selling costs (agent commissions, closing costs, staging)
- Net sale proceeds

Your CPA needs this to calculate your gain or loss on each flip — and to determine whether you're reporting as a dealer (ordinary income) or an investor (capital gains).

---

### ✅ 7. Update Your Depreciation Schedule

Any property you acquired, improved, or disposed of during the year needs to be reflected in your depreciation schedule. New purchases need a cost segregation or at least a land vs. improvement allocation. Disposals need their accumulated depreciation removed.

---

### ✅ 8. Record All Year-End Adjusting Entries

Depending on your accounting method:
- Prepaid expenses (insurance premiums paid in December for next year)
- Accrued expenses (December property taxes paid in January)
- Security deposits held at year-end (should be a liability, not income)

---

### ✅ 9. Run and Review Your Financial Reports

Before handing anything to your CPA, pull these reports in QuickBooks and review them for obvious errors:
- Profit & Loss by Property (every property should show activity)
- Balance Sheet (loan balances should match statements)
- Transaction Detail by Account (spot-check any large or unusual items)

If something looks wrong in a report, it is wrong. Fix it before your CPA sees it.

---

### ✅ 10. Organize Your Paper Trail

For any significant deduction, you should have a document to back it up. For the year-end file, organize:
- Contractor invoices and receipts over $500
- Loan statements (beginning and year-end balances)
- Property tax statements
- Insurance declarations pages
- Settlement statements for any acquisitions or dispositions

---

## What Happens If You Don't Do This

You hand your CPA a QuickBooks file with hundreds of uncategorized transactions, a pile of invoices, and a note that says "I think I paid the HVAC guy around $8,000."

They spend 6 hours sorting it out at $300/hour. You miss the Section 179 election because no one caught that the new water heater qualified. The depreciation schedule has errors because three properties weren't updated. You file late and pay a penalty.

Clean books aren't a nice-to-have. They're the difference between a tax season that costs you $700 and one that costs you $3,000.
